Case Study: The Hidden Cost of a $5 T-Shirt

This case follows a typical fast-fashion T-shirt sold at a very low price. Details differ by brand, but the pattern is common: fast production, low supplier margins, and high sales volume.

🏷️ Step 1: Price Pressure Starts at the Store

Global retailers compete on speed and price. To keep costs low, brands place large orders and negotiate hard with factories in countries such as Bangladesh, Vietnam, India, and Cambodia. For a $5 shirt, the factory might receive less than a dollar, leaving mere pennies for actual labor after material and overhead costs are deducted.

🧵 Step 2: Worker Impact in Production Hubs

Under this model, wages can stay low and overtime can increase. Workers may face unstable schedules, weak bargaining power, and conditions that do not match a living wage. In many garment-producing regions, minimum wages fall far short of a living wage. Workers often endure 10 to 12-hour shifts in unsafe conditions just to afford basic necessities like food and shelter for their families.

🏭 Step 3: Environmental Cost Is Externalized

Cotton and textile processing require large water and chemical use. Weak wastewater treatment can pollute rivers, and synthetic fabrics shed microfibers. The shirt stays cheap because these costs are externalized. It takes an estimated 2,700 liters of water to produce the cotton for a single T-shirt, roughly what one person drinks in two and a half years.

🗑️ Step 4: Disposal and National Waste Burden

Cheap shirts are more likely to be discarded quickly. At national scale, this raises textile waste, landfill pressure, and municipal disposal costs. The EPA estimates that over 11 million tons of textiles end up in US landfills every year. Because many modern shirts contain synthetic blends like polyester, they can take hundreds of years to decompose.

Conclusion of the Case

The case shows the butterfly effect clearly: one low-cost purchase is small, but repeated at scale, it supports a system that hides labor, environmental, and waste costs. Awareness can shift behavior over time.
